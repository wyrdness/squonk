## Overview

The squonk is a creature of the lumberjack 'fearsome critters' tradition of the late 19th and early 20th century United States. First described in print in William T. Cox's 1910 Fearsome Creatures of the Lumberwoods, with a Few Desert and Mountain Beasts, the squonk is said to inhabit the hemlock forests of northern Pennsylvania. Its skin is described as ill-fitting, covered in warts and moles, so that the creature is utterly miserable about its appearance and weeps perpetually. The squonk is therefore most easily tracked at night by following its tear-stained trail. The most famous tale, given by Cox and attributed to a hunter named J. P. Wentling of Pennsylvania, recounts how Wentling supposedly captured a squonk in a sack only to discover, upon arrival home, that the creature had dissolved into a puddle of tears and bubbles. The squonk has no zoological reality but has retained an outsized place in American folklore and popular culture, including a song by Genesis (1976), references in Steely Dan, and the annual Squonkapalooza festival in Johnstown, Pennsylvania.

> **Location**: Hemlock forest of north-central Pennsylvania  
> **Witnesses**: 1 (J. P. Wentling (alleged hunter))  
Cox (1910) reports that a Pennsylvania hunter named J. P. Wentling tracked a squonk by its tear-trail, lured it into a sack at dusk, and shouldered the bag homeward. On arriving home Wentling found the sack contained only a pool of tears and bubbles where the squonk had dissolved itself. This is the canonical tale of the species.
